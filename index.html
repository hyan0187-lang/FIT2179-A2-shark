Section 01 — Wait, Sharks Aren't #1?

The Myth

Every time you step into the ocean, there's that split-second thought — what if something's down there? Sharks have been living rent-free in our heads since Jaws hit cinemas in 1975. But here's the thing: Australia has a lot of animals that want a piece of you — and sharks are pretty far down that list.

Dogs Bite Harder

In 2021–22, animal-related hospitalisations in Australia told a story nobody expected. Dogs alone sent over 12,000 people to hospital — that's roughly 23 times more than all marine animals combined. Wasps, horses, even kangaroos all outranked sharks in sheer damage done. The ocean's most infamous predator? Barely a blip.

Most People Walk Away

Of the 1,283 shark incidents recorded in Australia since 1791, only 259 were fatal — that's about 1 in 5. The majority of people who encountered a shark either swam away injured, or walked away completely unharmed. Terrifying? Sure. Guaranteed death sentence? Far from it.

Section 02 — So Where Are They Hiding?

Sharks in Australia

So sharks aren't the killing machines we thought. But let's be honest — they're still out there. Australia has recorded shark incidents dating back to 1791, and over the past two centuries, the data shows some places are a lot riskier than others. Where you swim matters.

Click a state on the left map to filter incident locations on the right. Red dots mark fatal incidents.

NSW and QLD together account for over 65% of all recorded incidents — eastern seaboard density and surf culture drive the numbers.

But Western Australia has the highest fatality rate despite fewer total incidents. Remote coastlines mean help arrives too late when things go wrong.

The dot map reveals a dense coastal band along the east, with fatal red dots off WA disproportionate to the total count there.

The Numbers Are Rising

Shark incidents aren't a relic of the past — if anything, the numbers have been creeping up. The biggest spike on record? 2020, with 37 incidents — right when COVID lockdowns sent everyone rushing to the beach the moment restrictions lifted. More people in the water means more encounters. It's that simple.

Section 03 — Are You Sharks' Favourite Meal?

Who's Most at Risk?

Sharks don't discriminate — but the data does. After analysing over 760 recorded victims with known age and gender, a remarkably consistent pattern emerges: shark attacks are not random. Your age, sex, the season, and what you're doing in the water all shape your odds more than most people realise.

Each dot represents one incident. Yellow = Male, Pink = Female. The x-axis shows age (0–80); the y-axis separates males (above) from females (below) the centre line.

The top-left quadrant is strikingly dense: young males aged roughly 15–35 account for 487 incidents — nearly 4× more than any other age-gender group. The dots cluster so tightly they almost merge, which is a clear statistical signal, not a coincidence.

Females (below the line) make up only 11% of all recorded victims. Their incidents are spread more evenly across age groups, with no single dominant cluster. The data suggests the real risk driver is not swimming itself — it is who swims, how aggressively, and where.

What Were They Doing?

Swimming (470) and boarding (281) together account for 59% of all incidents — the two most common beach activities are also the two highest-risk ones. Meanwhile, spearfishing and snorkelling each contribute ~10% despite being niche activities, suggesting a disproportionately high risk per participant.

Gender Gap

Males make up 89% of all victims. The pyramid shows male dominance across every age bracket — most extreme in ages 10–29, where 393 males were recorded vs only 52 females.

When Is Most Dangerous?

January leads with 231 incidents — the highest of any month. Dec–Feb together total 551 incidents (43%) of all attacks, driven by Australia's beach season. Winter months (Jun–Aug) drop to just 47–63 — the safest window.

Section 04 — Which Shark Should You Actually Fear?

Meet the Sharks

You now know who gets attacked. But what's actually doing the attacking? Australia has over 180 species of sharks — but when it comes to serious encounters, three species are responsible for almost everything.

The Deadly Three

Look at the gap between fatal and non-fatal attacks for each species. White, Tiger, and Bull sharks are the only species with significant fatal numbers. Every other species on this list? Mostly just a nasty bite and a good story to tell at the pub.

The Wobbegong? 215 attacks, zero fatalities. Aggressive, yes. Lethal, not so much.

Every Fatal Attack Since 1960

Plot every death by year and victim age — the pattern is unmistakable. White sharks dominate in sheer volume, but Tiger sharks kill most efficiently per encounter.

The 2011 and 2021 clusters stand out — both years saw four or more fatalities. Notice the victims span all ages from 12 to 84, but the majority fall between 15 and 50.

Since 2010, white shark fatalities have accelerated — more dots, and increasingly spread to newer decades. Bull sharks account for the most recent deaths (2023, 2025), moving into river mouths and populated coastal areas.

Section 05 — How Do You Not Get Eaten?

Stay Safe

The good news? Knowing where, when, and what you're dealing with goes a long way. Here's what the data says about staying safe.

The Rankings Have Shifted

State-by-state rankings have shifted dramatically over the decades. NSW held rank #1 through the 1960s, 2000s, and 2010s — Australia's most populated coastline, most surfers, most encounters.

Queensland surged to the top through the 1970s–90s, driven by reef tourism and tropical water activities. Western Australia spent decades mid-table — then overtook everyone by the 2020s. Remote coastlines, fewer witnesses, no lifeguards. When things go wrong in WA, they go very wrong.

SA, VIC, and TAS have stayed consistently lower — colder water, fewer beachgoers, calmer conditions.

"Sharks aren't hunting you. In 234 years of recorded data, 1,283 incidents across an entire continent is genuinely not that many. But if you're a young male, planning to go surfing or swimming, on a warm January morning, somewhere off the Western Australian coast — maybe just check the conditions first. The ocean's beautiful. It just doesn't owe you anything."
